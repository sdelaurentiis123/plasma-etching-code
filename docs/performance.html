Performance & SOTA: the speedup is real, and it is CPU-dependent

petch is fully GPU-resident, so its wall-clock is ~1 s regardless of host. ViennaPS's level set runs on the CPU, so the ratio between them depends on the CPU you give ViennaPS. The honest range is ~8–15×, and ~8× is the floor.

The key idea

petch's whole pipeline — flux, level-set advection, fast-sweeping reinitialization, marching cubes — is GPU-resident, so its wall-clock is ~1 s and essentially CPU-independent. ViennaPS only puts its Monte-Carlo flux on the GPU (OptiX); its level-set advection runs on the CPU (a sparse HRLE structure) and is ~40% of its time. So the petch/ViennaPS speedup ratio is set by the CPU you hand ViennaPS: give it a fast many-core box and it shrinks toward petch (~8×); give it a weak box and ViennaPS's CPU side balloons (40–60×, an artifact). The stable, honest fact is the numerator: petch ≈ 1 s.

1. The honest, depth-matched benchmark

Measured on a 32-core / RTX 3090 box — deliberately giving ViennaPS a fast CPU, the conservative case — both engines warmed, both run to a matched etch depth (within 3–7%), swept across hole diameters:

Reproduce: scripts/vps_sweep.py (the rate range is set so petch actually depth-matches ViennaPS). The cross-validation page has the full like-for-like accuracy comparison on ViennaPS Validation §6.

Honesty caveats kept on the record
  • An early "~23×" was a GPU-vs-CPU artifact (our GPU vs ViennaPS's CPU engine). Retracted.
  • A later flat "~14×" was real but came from a mid-CPU box presented as universal. The ratio is CPU-dependent; ~14× is the typical-CPU point, ~8× is the fast-CPU floor, and the 40–60× seen on a weak 6-vCPU box is the inflation trap, not a gain.

2. Why petch is GPU-resident — what the campaign shipped

petch reaches ~1 s by keeping the entire loop on the GPU. Each change below is a per-stage self-speedup (an internal acceleration of petch's own loop, not a ViennaPS ratio), and each is exact — no accuracy traded for speed.

StageChangeEffect
reinitnarrow-band, then GPU fast-sweeping Godunov–Eikonal (reinit_method="fsm")~58× on the reinit line; \(|\nabla\varphi|=1\) exact at the fixed point
coveragewarm-started coverage fixed point (warm_start_coverage)1 iteration instead of ~4 → ~8× fewer neutral ray traces, same converged result
advectWarp kernel, \(\varphi\) resident across all CFL substeps~3–4 s → 0.1 s; matches numpy to \(10^{-6}\)
meshGPU marching cubes (wp.MarchingCubes)~24× faster than skimage
fluxon-device normalize + 1-neighbour smoothing, Sobol QMC source1 readback/step; variance ~\(1/N\)

With these, the loop is flux-throughput bound and CPU host ops are off the critical path — which is exactly why petch's wall-clock no longer tracks the CPU. The original diagnosis was right: we never needed a faster ray tracer (the flux kernel is RT-core-class, ~200 M rays/s); we needed to stop doing dense per-step work the sparse reference avoids, and to keep the field on the device.

3. SOTA roadmap — widen the lead, on accuracy not just speed

Speed is no longer the bottleneck; the remaining levers are about being better, not just faster.

Deterministic neutral transport (shipped, more to calibrate)

The flux loop's noise comes from the many-bounce neutral Monte-Carlo. petch now also carries a deterministic discrete-ordinates (Sn) neutral transport solver (a Warp kernel, ported from the sibling plasma_sim): it marches a fixed cosine-weighted set of directions through the level-set grid, noise-free, and reproduces ViennaPS's ARDE (0.727 vs 0.73 at AR 8.6) without the Monte-Carlo deep-floor starvation — at ~0.1 s per evaluation on CUDA. Two further deterministic neutral options are ported but need calibration (a 1-D Knudsen-conductance tail and a GMRES radiosity solve). See Flux & Transport and ViennaPS Validation §7. (Note: this is the Sn transport solver, distinct from the "DDA" voxel-traversal used for ray-surface hits.)

The rest of the level-set core

GPU Eikonal via the Fast Iterative Method (Jeong & Whitaker) — block-based, lock-free, the right GPU primitive vs sequential heap-based fast-marching; keep the marching-cubes mesh + RT-core BVH (direct DDA voxel-marching loses ≥3× on throughput, Manstetten, and accuracy at grazing angles, Riedel); and WENO5 + Stencil-Local Lax–Friedrichs (Toifl 2020) for the non-convex sputter Hamiltonian.

Framework: NVIDIA Warp

The whole pipeline is NVIDIA Warp: GPU-resident level-set kernels, RT-core ray tracing through wp.Mesh, and — uniquely among the options — it keeps autodiff. Every speedup above stays differentiable, the structural edge ViennaPS does not have (see Differentiable & ML).

The edge is all three at once

petch is ~8–15× faster than ViennaPS-GPU (CPU-dependent; ~1 s GPU-resident vs ViennaPS's CPU-bound 7–64 s), as accurate (replicates ViennaPS's ballistic model; matches on holes, ~0.06–0.1 gentler on trenches), and differentiable (full wp.Tape gradients from outcome back to recipe). Any one alone is matchable; the three together is the edge — and the live frontier is now matching the real wafer, not ViennaPS.
