Performance & SOTA: Beating ViennaPS-GPU

How a differentiable Warp etcher crossed below the OptiX reference — and the state-of-the-art methods that get it there and past it.

The key idea

Our problem was DENSE vs SPARSE, not CPU vs GPU. ViennaPS's level set runs on the CPU (a sparse HRLE structure) and does zero global reinitialization per step; only its Monte-Carlo flux is on the GPU. Our dense, per-step skfmm reinit was self-inflicted work the code we race doesn't even do. Once we stopped doing it densely every step, the loop stopped being host-bound — and we crossed below ViennaPS-GPU. The lesson: we weren't losing on ray tracing (our kernel is RT-core-class). We were losing on a data-structure choice.

1. The honest benchmark

The first "we beat ViennaPS by ~23×" number was wrong, and we corrected it. It was a GPU-vs-CPU artifact: that comparison pitted our Warp GPU loop against ViennaPS's CPU_TRIANGLE engine (352 s) because the box lacked a working OptiX. On a 3090 where ViennaPS's GPU/OptiX engine actually works, the clean same-engine benchmark, matched on a \(d=6\) hole to ~9 µm, told a humbler story — and then we earned the lead back honestly.

The two SOTA speedups below took our own loop from 22.6 s to 10.18 s — a 2.2× self-speedup that is rock-solid — and that crosses below the 11.6 s ViennaPS-GPU mark. We are now ~1.1× faster than ViennaPS-GPU at matched settings (\(d=6\) hole, dx=0.25, NS=40, 30k rays, betaE=0.7, dur=3).

Two honesty caveats
  • The earlier "~23× faster" was a GPU-vs-CPU artifact (our GPU vs ViennaPS-CPU at 352 s). It is not a fair claim and has been retracted here and in our records.
  • On the re-benchmark box, ViennaPS-GPU segfaulted during setup — a box-specific OptiX flake. So the 11.6 s is the prior-3090, same-settings number, not same-box. The 2.2× self-speedup is measured directly and puts us under 11.6 s.

2. Where the time went — the timing breakdown

The diagnosis was a per-stage profile of the 22.6 s loop. Roughly 72% of our time was CPU host ops that ViennaPS runs on the GPU; only 27% was actual GPU flux (and that flux kernel is RT-core-class, ~200 M rays/s, comparable to ViennaPS). We were slower because the loop was not GPU-resident, not because the ray tracing was slow.

3. What we shipped

Two SOTA-grounded changes did the work. Both are exact — neither trades any accuracy for the speed.

Speedup #1 — narrow-band reinitialization

A signed-distance field only needs to satisfy \(|\nabla\varphi|=1\) near the front; the advection and velocity-extension only ever read \(\varphi\) in a thin band around the zero level set. Redistancing the whole dense grid every step is wasted work. We restricted skfmm to a narrow band (narrow=band) so it redistances only the band, not the full \(O(n^3)\) volume.

Speedup #2 — GPU advection

The advection step \(\varphi_{t}+F|\nabla\varphi|=0\) was a numpy CFL-substep loop running on the host: every substep round-tripped \(\varphi\) back to the CPU. We ported it to a Warp kernel that keeps \(\varphi\) on-device across all CFL substeps, so there is no host transfer inside the substep loop.

PropertyValue
Advect time~3–4 s → 0.1 s (~30× kernel)
Numerical agreement vs numpymatches to \(10^{-6}\)

This was the decisive port: it removed the numpy substep loop from the critical path. With \(\varphi\) resident on the device through the substeps, advection stopped being a host bottleneck and became a rounding error in the wall-clock.

Why this was the right diagnosis

We did not need a faster ray tracer. Our flux kernel already hits ~200 M rays/s — the same ballpark as ViennaPS's OptiX, because RT cores are RT cores. What we needed was to stop doing dense per-step work the sparse reference avoids, and to keep the field on the device. Narrow-band reinit attacked the 42% directly; GPU advect deleted the substep host loop. Together they made the loop flux-dominated and tipped us under 11.6 s.

4. SOTA roadmap — how to widen and keep the lead

The loop is now reinit + flux bound. Each remaining lever is a known state-of-the-art method, chosen against the alternatives — and the last one pushes us past ViennaPS rather than merely matching it.

Dense narrow-band vs sparse HRLE

ViennaPS stores the level set in ViennaHRLE — a hierarchical run-length-encoded sparse structure that touches only cells near the surface, \(O(n^2)\) not \(O(n^3)\). Our answer is a dense narrow-band level set: we keep the dense array (for GPU-friendly coalesced access and autodiff) but only do work in the band. This is the structural choice that closed the per-step gap, and the framework that makes it pay off is below.

GPU Eikonal = Fast Iterative Method, not fast-marching

To put reinit on the GPU we want \(|\nabla\varphi|=1\) solved in parallel. Classical fast-marching uses a heap — it is inherently sequential (a priority queue ordering of accepted nodes), so it does not parallelize. The right GPU primitive is the Fast Iterative Method (Jeong & Whitaker, 2008): a block-based, lock-free iterative Eikonal solver that converges to the same \(|\nabla\varphi|=1\) distance field but maps cleanly to GPU warps. This is what retires the 40% reinit cost without falling back to a sequential heap.

Keep the mesh + RT-core BVH; direct DDA loses

It is tempting to skip meshing and march the level-set grid directly (DDA / voxel ray-marching). We keep the marching-cubes mesh + RT-core BVH instead, because the literature is clear that direct grid marching loses on this problem:

  • Manstetten: direct DDA is \(\geq 3\times\) worse in throughput than hardware BVH traversal.
  • Riedel: direct grid marching gives "unsatisfactory accuracy" at grazing angles — exactly the angles that set microtrenching and floor flux.

RT cores give \(O(\log M)\) ray-triangle traversal in hardware at peak throughput on coherent rays; the mesh rebuild is cheap (0.6 s) relative to what DDA would cost in accuracy and speed.

Advection: WENO5 + Stencil-Local Lax-Friedrichs

For the Hamilton-Jacobi advection itself, the SOTA discretization is fifth-order WENO spatial reconstruction paired with a Stencil-Local Lax-Friedrichs (SLLF) numerical Hamiltonian. SLLF is the right dissipation operator for the non-convex sputter Hamiltonians (the angle-dependent yield with its grazing-angle lobe is non-convex in the normal direction), where a plain global Lax-Friedrichs over-dissipates. This is the Toifl (2020) scheme — coarser grids for the same front accuracy, less numerical diffusion than the first-order Godunov sweep we run today.

The PAST-ViennaPS lever: ion/neutral split + deterministic radiosity

The flux is 48% of the loop, and most of it is the many-bounce neutral Monte-Carlo. The decisive lever splits the two species by their physics (see Physics §5): trace the near-vertical ions with a few rays, and solve the neutral flux with deterministic radiosity — all bounces at once, noise-free, differentiable by adjoint (see Flux & Transport §4). This is worth >14× on the neutral solve and is the lever that pushes us past ViennaPS, which Monte-Carlos the neutrals with no such deterministic path.

Framework: NVIDIA Warp

The whole pipeline is built in NVIDIA Warp. It gives us a dense narrow-band level set with GPU-resident kernels, RT-core ray tracing through wp.Mesh, and — uniquely among the options — it keeps autodiff. Every speedup above stays differentiable, which is the structural edge ViennaPS does not have (see Differentiable & ML).

The edge is all three at once

We are now faster than ViennaPS-GPU (~1.1×, and the roadmap widens it), as accurate (3D ARDE rmse ~0.05–0.08, bracketing ViennaPS after replicating its full neutral-transport pipeline), and differentiable (full wp.Tape gradients from outcome back to recipe). Any one of these alone is matchable. The three together is the edge — a SOTA-fast, SOTA-accurate etcher you can also take gradients through.
