The Physics of Plasma Etching at the Feature Scale

From the plasma sheath to the surface reaction that moves the wall by one nanometer.

1. The multiscale picture: where "feature scale" sits

Plasma etching spans an enormous range of length scales, and we model only the smallest one:

ScaleLengthWhat happensModeled here?
Reactor / plasma bulk0.1–1 melectron impact, ionization, radical generationNo — gives us boundary data
Sheath0.1–1 mmions accelerated toward the wafer; sets ion energy/angleNo — gives us \(I_{\rm ion}(E,\theta)\)
Feature10 nm – 10 µmballistic transport into the trench, surface reactions, shape changeYes — this is the whole simulator

The plasma and sheath enter our problem only as boundary conditions at the top of the feature: the incident fluxes of each species, the ion energy distribution (IED), and the ion angular distribution (IAD). Everything below the feature mouth is ours to compute.

2. The sheath: why ions are a beam and neutrals are a fog

At the plasma–wafer boundary a thin space-charge sheath forms. Positive ions entering it are accelerated across a potential drop \(V_{\rm sheath}\) (tens to hundreds of volts), gaining directed energy \(E \approx eV_{\rm sheath}\) normal to the wafer. This is why ions arrive nearly vertical and energetic.

Derivation — ion angular spread

An ion enters the sheath with a small thermal transverse velocity \(v_\perp\sim\sqrt{k_BT_i/m}\) and is accelerated to a large normal velocity \(v_\parallel=\sqrt{2E/m}\). The arrival angle from vertical is

$$\tan\theta \;=\; \frac{v_\perp}{v_\parallel}\;\sim\;\sqrt{\frac{k_BT_i}{2E}}.$$

With \(k_BT_i\sim 0.05\) eV and \(E\sim 100\) eV, \(\theta\lesssim 1.5^\circ\). Hence the few-degree angular spread used for ions (the reference code uses \(\sigma_{\rm ion}=2.5^\circ\)). The IED is often bimodal in RF plasmas (ions sample the oscillating sheath voltage), which matters when we integrate the yield over energy.

Neutral radicals (F, O) are not accelerated — they are thermal. They arrive from the plasma with a near-isotropic, cosine-law (Lambertian) angular distribution. So at the feature mouth we have two utterly different populations: a vertical energetic beam (ions) and a diffuse thermal fog (neutrals).

3. Transport regime: why flight is ballistic

Inside the feature, do particles collide with gas molecules? The decider is the Knudsen number \(\mathrm{Kn}=\lambda/L\), the ratio of gas mean free path to feature size.

Numbers

At RIE pressures (\(\sim\)10 mTorr) the mean free path \(\lambda\sim\) mm–cm. A feature is \(L\sim\)µm. So

$$\mathrm{Kn}=\frac{\lambda}{L}\sim\frac{10^{-3}\,\text{m}}{10^{-6}\,\text{m}}=10^{3}\gg1.$$
Consequence

\(\mathrm{Kn}\gg1\) means transport inside the feature is collisionless and straight-line (ballistic / free-molecular). Particles fly in straight lines from the mouth until they hit a wall. There is no gas-phase scattering term, and — crucially — ions and neutrals never interact in flight. They meet only at the surface. This is what makes the species-split solver (Flux & Transport) exact.

4. The flux integral

The local flux of a species onto a surface point \(\mathbf{x}\) is the incident angular flux integrated over the visible hemisphere, weighted by the cosine of incidence and by visibility:

$$\Gamma(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \int_{\Omega} I(\boldsymbol\omega)\,\big(\boldsymbol\omega\cdot\mathbf{n}\big)\,V(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol\omega)\;d\boldsymbol\omega.$$

Here \(V(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol\omega)=1\) if the source (or a re-emitting wall) is unobstructed along \(\boldsymbol\omega\), else \(0\). That visibility factor is shadowing — the geometric origin of aspect-ratio-dependent etching. Computing this integral is the entire job of the transport layer.

The Knudsen cosine law (neutral source), derived

Derivation — why neutrals arrive \(\propto\cos\theta\)

For a gas in equilibrium effusing through the feature mouth, the number of molecules crossing into solid angle \(d\Omega\) at polar angle \(\theta\) is proportional to the projected area of the aperture seen from that direction, i.e. \(\propto\cos\theta\). In 3D the emitted distribution is \(p(\theta)\,d\Omega\propto\cos\theta\,d\Omega\); sampled by \(\cos\theta=\sqrt{U_1},\ \phi=2\pi U_2\). In 2D it collapses to

$$p(\theta)=\tfrac12\cos\theta,\quad \theta\in[-\tfrac\pi2,\tfrac\pi2],$$

sampled by \(\theta=\arcsin(U)\) with \(U\) uniform on \([-1,1]\). (Note: the 3D sampling rule \(\sqrt{U}\) is wrong in 2D but becomes correct in 3D — a subtle bug in the 2D reference code that the 3D port silently fixes.)

5. Sticking and re-emission

When a neutral hits a wall it does not always react. With probability \(s\) (the sticking coefficient) it adsorbs/reacts; with probability \(1-s\) it desorbs and re-emits diffusely (another cosine lobe about the local normal) and flies on. Typical values are small: \(s_F\approx0.2\), \(s_O\approx0.3\). Low sticking means a neutral may bounce many times, which is how flux reaches the bottom of a deep feature — and is the entire reason neutral transport is a multiple-scattering problem rather than a single-shot one.

Ions, by contrast, have effective sticking \(\approx1\): they deposit their energy on first contact (etch or sputter), with at most a single specular reflection at grazing incidence. So the two species sit at opposite ends of the "how many bounces" axis — the axis that decides the right numerical method.

6. Surface chemistry: yields

An energetic ion striking the surface removes material. The number of atoms removed per ion is the yield \(Y\). Two facts shape it:

Energy dependence: the threshold square-root law

Below a threshold energy \(E_{\rm th}\) the ion cannot displace a surface atom and \(Y=0\). Above it, the semi-empirical Steinbrüchel form (rooted in linear collision-cascade sputter theory near threshold) is linear in \(\sqrt{E}\):

$$Y(E)=A\big(\sqrt{E}-\sqrt{E_{\rm th}}\big)_+ .$$

Three channels are modeled, each with its own \((A,E_{\rm th})\): ion-enhanced chemical etch (\(Y_{ie}\)), physical sputter of bare material (\(Y_{sp}\)), and ion removal of the passivation layer (\(Y_p\)).

Bias contributor #2 — Jensen gap

Evaluating \(Y\) at the mean energy \(\langle E\rangle\) is not the same as the energy-averaged yield \(\langle Y(E)\rangle\). Because \(\sqrt{\cdot}\) is concave, Jensen's inequality gives \(\langle Y(E)\rangle<Y(\langle E\rangle)\) — a systematic over-estimate. For a narrow IED (\(\sigma=10\) at \(E=100\)) the gap is tiny (\(\sim0.2\%\)); for a broad or bimodal IED it is not. Fix: integrate \(Y\) over the real IED.

Angular dependence: the source of microtrenching

Sputter yield is not monotonic in incidence angle. It rises from normal incidence to a peak at oblique angles (\(\sim60\text{–}70^\circ\)) as ions deposit momentum more efficiently into the surface plane, then collapses to zero near grazing. A simple forward-peaked \(\cos\theta\) (as in the reference code) misses this structure. The grazing-angle lobe is what concentrates removal at the bottom corners of a trench, carving the characteristic microtrenches. ViennaPS encodes it with an inflection near \(89^\circ\) and a steep exponent.

7. Ion–neutral synergy: the heart of reactive ion etching

Coburn–Winters (1979)

The canonical experiment: XeF\(_2\) gas alone etches silicon slowly; Ar\(^+\) ions alone sputter slowly; together they etch roughly an order of magnitude faster than the sum. Neutrals build a reactive (fluorinated) layer; ions supply the energy to remove it. Neither works alone. This synergy is reactive ion etching, and it is the only place ions and neutrals couple.

Competitive-Langmuir coverage model, derived

The surface is partitioned into fractional coverages: fluorinated \(\theta_F\), oxygen-passivated \(\theta_O\), and bare. Fluorine and oxygen compete for the same sites. Write the steady-state balance for fluorine — adsorption onto bare sites versus ion-driven removal:

Derivation $$\frac{d\theta_F}{dt}=s_F\Gamma_F\,(\text{bare}) - Y_{ie}\Gamma_{\rm ion}\,\theta_F = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \frac{\theta_F}{\text{bare}}=\frac{s_F\Gamma_F}{Y_{ie}\Gamma_{\rm ion}}\equiv r_F.$$

Likewise \(r_O = s_O\Gamma_O/(Y_p\Gamma_{\rm ion})\). Close with site conservation \(1=\text{bare}+\theta_F+\theta_O=\text{bare}(1+r_F+r_O)\):

$$\boxed{\ \text{bare}=\frac{1}{1+r_F+r_O},\qquad \theta_F=r_F\cdot\text{bare}\ }$$

The etch rate (normal velocity) sums the two removal channels — ion-enhanced etch of the fluorinated layer plus physical sputter of bare material — divided by the atomic number density \(\rho\) to convert a removed-atom flux into a recession speed:

$$V_n=\frac{1}{\rho}\Big(Y_{ie}\,\Gamma_{\rm ion}\,\theta_F + Y_{sp}\,\Gamma_{\rm ion}\,\text{bare}\Big).$$

Notice the dominant term \(V_n\sim Y_{ie}\Gamma_{\rm ion}\theta_F\) with \(\theta_F\sim\Gamma_F/\Gamma_{\rm ion}\): it is a product of an ion quantity and a neutral quantity. That multiplicative coupling is the synergy, and it lives entirely in this one expression.

Bias contributor #1 — the dominant one

This clean steady-state ratio is the original PoC simplification of ViennaPS's exact Belen/Ertl form, which solves coupled coverage ODEs with extra redeposition / coupling terms (\(k_\sigma,\beta_\sigma\)) and a coverage-dependent sticking \(s(\theta)\). petch now implements that exact Belen form — so this is a model-form note, not the headline gap. With it, petch's ARDE matches ViennaPS on holes and is ~0.06–0.1 gentler (flatter, not steeper) on trenches — a converged source-discretization difference, see ViennaPS Validation. The first-order residual is structural (the ballistic-vs-wafer gap), not this ~7% chemistry detail.

8. Where the coupling reaches back into transport

If sticking is constant, the two transports are fully independent and recombine only in the velocity formula above. But in the real model \(s=s(\theta)\), and \(\theta\) depends on the ion flux. So the neutral transport's boundary condition depends on the ion solution — a genuine ion↔neutral coupling. It is resolved by a cheap self-consistent fixed point per timestep:

guess θ
repeat:
    solve neutral transport with s(θ)   →  Γ_F, Γ_O
    solve ion transport                 →  Γ_ion      (few rays)
    update θ from chemistry             →  new θ
until θ converges            # 2–4 iterations; geometry/operator K reused

Because the expensive geometry (visibility, form-factor operator \(K\)) is frozen during the step, each iteration only re-solves a cheap linear system with an updated diagonal. See Flux & Transport for the operator.

9. Emergent phenomena (what the physics produces)

PhenomenonMechanism
ARDE / RIE lagNarrow/deep features subtend a smaller solid angle to the source, so flux (especially neutrals) falls with aspect ratio — deeper features etch slower. Pure geometry + shadowing; falls out parameter-free.
BowingExcess sidewall flux (ion reflection, charging) widens the mid-depth. A 3D-NAND failure mode.
MicrotrenchingGrazing-angle sputter lobe + ion reflection concentrate removal at bottom corners.
Twisting / tiltingAsymmetric charging / ion deflection in very high-aspect-ratio holes.
FacetingAngle-dependent yield erodes mask corners into facets.
Why ARDE is worse for holes than trenches

A 2D trench of depth \(d\) and width \(w\) sees a source solid angle that shrinks roughly like \(w/d\). A 3D circular hole sees an angle that shrinks roughly like \((w/d)^2\) — the opening contracts in two dimensions at once. So aspect-ratio limitation bites harder in 3D contact/channel holes than in trenches. This is exactly why 3D-NAND channel-hole etch is the defining hard problem.
