Flux & Transport: The Computational Heart

The transport integral, the three ways to solve it, the ion/neutral solver split, and DDA vs. hardware ray tracing.

This is the layer that consumes ~94% of the runtime in the reference code and is the entire reason a GPU is in the plan. Everything here is about evaluating one integral — the flux \(\Gamma(\mathbf{x})\) on every surface element — as fast and as smoothly as possible.

1. The transport equation

Generalizing the flux integral to include re-emission, the outgoing surface flux distribution satisfies a Fredholm integral equation of the second kind:

$$\Gamma_{\rm out}(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol\omega_0)=\underbrace{\Gamma_{\rm src}(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol\omega_0)}_{\text{direct from source}}+\underbrace{\int_{\Omega} F_{\rm re}(\boldsymbol\omega_0,\boldsymbol\omega)\,\Gamma_{\rm in}(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol\omega)\,d\boldsymbol\omega}_{\text{re-emitted from other walls}}.$$

The kernel \(F_{\rm re}\) encodes the re-emission law (and may change species or energy — e.g. an ion sputtering surface atoms). For the common diffuse case (isotropic re-emission, sticking \(s\)) this reduces to a radiosity-type equation. There are exactly three families of method to solve it.

The view (form) factor

The geometric core of radiosity is the fraction of radiation leaving patch \(j\) that arrives at patch \(i\):

$$F_{ji}=\frac{1}{\pi A_j}\iint_{A_i,A_j}\frac{\cos\theta_i\cos\theta_j}{r_{ij}^2}\;V_{ij}\;dA_i\,dA_j,$$

with \(V_{ij}\) the patch-to-patch visibility. Assemble these into the operator \(K\) and the diffuse radiosity equation with sticking \(s\) is

$$\Gamma=\Gamma_{\rm direct}+(1-s)\,K\,\Gamma.$$

3. Why Monte Carlo is noisy — and why that matters

A Monte-Carlo estimate of the flux integral has standard error \(\propto\sigma/\sqrt{N}\), where \(\sigma\) is the variance of the integrand and \(N\) the hits per element. With only tens of hits per surface element, that is 10–20% noise. Two consequences:

  • The trench floor looks jagged — but it averages to the right answer. This is variance, not bias.
  • The noise corrupts gradients, which is fatal for the differentiable goal. You cannot backpropagate cleanly through a noisy flux field.

So the variance is not just cosmetic — killing it is a prerequisite for inverse design. This is the through-line of the Acceleration page.

4. The species split

The organizing insight

The right method is selected by the sticking coefficient, which sets how many bounces matter. \(s\to1\) is a single-shot beam problem (cheap, low-variance). \(s\to0\) is a diffuse equilibrium problem (radiosity). Ions and neutrals sit at opposite ends, so they want different solvers. The split is exact because ballistic flight is collisionless — they never interact until the surface (see Physics §3, §8).

Radiosity for neutrals: the Neumann series IS the bounce chain

Derivation — why the linear solve beats truncated bouncing

Expand the solve as a Neumann series:

$$\Gamma=\big(I-(1-s)K\big)^{-1}\Gamma_{\rm direct}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\big[(1-s)K\big]^n\Gamma_{\rm direct}.$$

Term \(n\) is exactly the \(n\)-th re-emission bounce. A Monte-Carlo run with a fixed cap of \(n_{\rm re}=12\) bounces computes only the first 12 terms — it truncates the tail. The series converges at rate \(\sim(1-s)\,\rho(K)\), where \(\rho(K)\) measures how "closed" the feature is. In a deep, narrow hole \(\rho(K)\to1\) (little flux escapes), and with low sticking \((1-s)\to0.8\), convergence is slow — so the dropped tail is non-negligible. The matrix solve sums the whole series. Hence radiosity-for-neutrals is not only faster and noise-free, it is more accurate exactly in the high-aspect-ratio regime we care about.

Bonus: a linear solve is cleanly differentiable by the adjoint method (one extra solve), whereas stochastic bounce chains are noisy to differentiate. So the split also serves the differentiable goal.

Ions: barely any rays needed

A near-collimated, stick-on-contact beam has almost no angular variance, so the flux at a point is essentially "what fraction of the narrow arrival cone sees the source, times \(\cos\theta\)." For perfectly vertical ions it is a single shadow ray straight up. A few hundred QMC rays — or a closed-form visible-solid-angle integral — suffice, plus one deterministic specular bounce for grazing reflection. Spending 20k pseudorandom particles on ions (as the reference code does) is pure waste.

5. Traversal: DDA grid marching vs. BVH triangles

Whatever the method, you must answer "where does this ray first hit the surface?" millions of times. Two architectures:

DDA / sphere tracing on the level set

The surface is already a level set on a uniform grid, and it moves every step. So march the ray cell-by-cell through the \(\varphi\) grid (Amanatides–Woo voxel traversal) and detect the sign change \(\varphi:\ \text{gas}(<0)\to\text{solid}(>0)\). That crossing is the hit. The grid is the acceleration structure — no mesh extraction, no BVH, nothing to rebuild.

Sphere tracing — free acceleration from the SDF

Because \(\varphi\) is a signed-distance function (\(|\nabla\varphi|=1\)), the value \(|\varphi(\mathbf{x})|\) is a guaranteed-safe step length: nothing is closer than that. So step along the ray by \(|\varphi|\) each time (Hart's algorithm). Big strides through open gas, fine steps near walls — no fixed step size needed.

  • Pros: zero per-step build cost; SDF gives sphere-tracing for free; coherent, cache-friendly memory access; coherence-agnostic (good for incoherent diffuse neutral rays, where RT cores underperform).
  • Cons: accuracy tied to \(\Delta x\) (then interpolate \(\varphi\) sub-cell); sphere tracing degrades at grazing incidence (\(|\varphi|\) stays tiny → many tiny steps), which HARC sidewalls produce constantly — mitigate with relaxed sphere tracing or a fixed-step DDA fallback; a sparse narrow band stores valid distances only near the surface, so open-space stepping needs a coarse global distance or a "big-step-until-band, then DDA" hybrid; does not use the dedicated RT-core silicon.

BVH triangles + RT cores

Extract a triangle mesh, build a bounding-volume hierarchy, traverse in \(O(\log M)\) — accelerated in hardware by RT cores (ray-triangle is their native primitive). ViennaPS's OptiX path does this. Pros: peak ray throughput on coherent rays, sub-grid geometric precision. Cons: rebuild the BVH every step (geometry moves); RT cores hit peak only on coherent ray bundles, and diffuse re-emission is incoherent.

6. Shadowing falls out for free

Note there is never an explicit "visibility computation." In Monte Carlo, the nearest intersection along a ray is the shadow test — if a particle's first hit is a sidewall, the floor below simply never receives it. In radiosity, \(V_{ij}\) in the form factor is the same nearest-hit query between patches. Aspect-ratio-dependent etching is therefore an emergent geometric consequence, not a modeled effect.

The split changes how each flux is computed but not where species couple — so it introduces no new approximation beyond what every tool already makes by freezing the surface during a step. See Physics §8.
